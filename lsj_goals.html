Four core goals for the watershed

Goals were developed through water quality data analysis, regulatory context, and stakeholder priorities. Select any goal to learn more about targets, current conditions, and recommended management actions.

Goal 01 Sediment & salinity

Reduce erosion and runoff-driven sediment and salinity loading from priority source areas

Target ~30–35% TDS load reductions in impaired assessment units, focusing on Mancos Shale landscapes, ephemeral tributaries, and irrigated lands.

Goal 02 Bacteria

Improve bacteria conditions to support recreational, cultural, and aquatic life uses

Target ~20% reduction in peak E. coli concentrations at impaired reaches through livestock access management, riparian buffers, and community wastewater improvements.

Goal 03 Metals & legacy sites

Address locally elevated metals, selenium, and radionuclide concerns from legacy and natural sources

Target ~10% reduction in dissolved selenium loads in impaired reaches through abandoned mine stabilization, passive treatment systems, and coordination with ongoing remediation programs.

Goal 04 Hydrology & riparian

Improve hydrologic and riparian function to support water quality, habitat, and watershed resilience

Restore baseflows, native riparian vegetation, and channel stability using low-tech approaches (beaver dam analogs, tamarisk removal) across Bears Ears canyons, Abajo headwaters, and the San Juan riparian corridor.

Key stressors addressed by this plan

Sediment & turbidity

Event-driven runoff from Mancos Shale landscapes and ephemeral tributaries delivers disproportionate sediment loads downstream.

Bacteria (E. coli)

Livestock access, unsewered communities, and high-runoff events contribute to E. coli exceedances across recreational and aquatic life reaches.

Metals & radionuclides

Legacy uranium mines, oil field brine, selenium-rich shale soils, and the White Mesa Mill area contribute localized contamination risks.

Altered hydrology

Channel incision, tamarisk invasion, degraded riparian corridors, and reduced baseflows impair water quality and aquatic habitat across jurisdictions.

Goal 5: Strengthen monitoring & adaptive management

A fifth cross-cutting goal — building coordinated long-term monitoring, data integration, and adaptive management capacity — supports all four watershed goals above. This includes targeted surface water and groundwater monitoring, tribal and state data-sharing, community-based monitoring programs, and partnerships with academic institutions.
